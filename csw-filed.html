Independently Verifying the Facts

Paid Shill Confusion

Kurt Wuckert Jr. is paid by CoinGeek to be a "Bitcoin historian". On this issue, he wrote on Twitter (archive here):

Yes. He didn’t claim to own them. It was a set of addresses generated by someone else which he said he likely owned some of due to the dates they were generated. Everyone not being specifically malicious knows this.

This straightforward denial of publicly verifiable facts is a central trope of the BSV community's disinformation campaign. This first section aims to dispel the confusion they are trying to create by pointing directly to the publicly available sources.

Identifying the Right Document

For discovery in the Kleiman-Wright case Wright was asked to state his Bitcoin holdings on 31st December 2013. Wright avoided providing, and to date has never provided, this figure.

Instead, during discovery and settlement negotiations, Wright submitted several different, mostly partial, and sometimes conflicting lists of Bitcoin addresses he claimed to own.

Andreas Antonopoulos was called as an expert witness to scrutinise four of the lists produced by Wright. Antonopoulos gives labels to these four lists on pp. 20-21 of his report:

The four documents labelled are:

  • The "Shadders List": this is the document referred to by Kurt as the "set of addresses generated by [Craig's employee] which [Craig] said he likely owned".
  • The "CW" list: a set of addresses Wright gave in settlement talks that he claimed he owned (not publicly available)
  • The "DK" list: a set of addresses Wright gave in settlement talks that he claimed Dave Kleiman owned (not publicly available)
  • The "CSW Filed" list: this is the document containing the list of addresses that Wright has sworn on oath belong to him. This document is the one that Kurt is desperate to create public confusion about to avoid its implications.

Late in the discovery process, Wright introduced the claim that the Bitcoin addresses he owned were being kept for him by complex trust structures, and thus were inaccessible. His claims about these trusts were conflicting. Famously, he claimed that the method to access the addresses would be delivered to him in January 2020 by bonded courier (). The Magistrate judge concluded that Wright's story about the trust did not exonerate him from discovery violation and that Wright was being contradictory and deceptive, so he sanctioned Wright. The federal judged partly affirmed and partly reverted these sanctions on the condition that the bonded courier arrive by February 3rd 2020 with what Craig had claimed was the true list of Bitcoin addresses that he owned.

Documents and Quotes Showing That Wright Claimed the "CSW Filed" List Was a List of Bitcoin Addresses He Owned, and that Access to the Encrypted File Containing Them Was Delivered by a Bonded Courier

  • Wright's claims about the bonded courier were recorded in a transcript filed as item #236 in the court docket. The transcript is available to buy on Pacer, but has not yet been purchases and made publicly available.
  • The Magistrate judge describes Wright's claims about the encrypted file on p. 19 in this document:
    • Wright "put all his bitcoin (and/or the keys to it – his story changed) into a computer file that is encrypted with a hierarchical Shamir encryption protocol."
    • "He then put the encrypted file into a “blind” trust (of which he is one of the trustees),"
    • "gave away a controlling number of the key slices to now-deceased David Kleiman, and therefore cannot now decrypt the file that controls access to the bitcoin."
    • "His only hope is that a bonded courier arrives on an unknown dated in January 2020 with the decryption keys."
  • In a written response to the Magistrate judge's order, Wright's lawyers make the following statements of relevance to this file and list of addresses:
    • "The algorithm necessary to generate a complete list of his Bitcoin public addresses is in an encrypted file that is protected by a Shamir encryption scheme." (p. 3)
    • "To unlock the encrypted file, Dr. Wright would need at least 8 of the 15 key slices, but he has only 7." (p. 3)
    • "His best friend, Dave Kleiman, set up a system whereby Dr. Wright would, at the earliest, start to receive the remaining key slices in January of 2020 by bonded courier, at which point, Dr. Wright would be able to comply with the Court’s order." (pp. 3-4)
    • "Dr. Wright is unable to circumvent this system to obtain the eighth key slice at an earlier date because he was not involved in setting up the courier system and because Dave Kleiman is no longer alive." (p. 4)
  • On the 10th January the federal judge gave her order with this statement: "In light of the Defendant’s representations that the bonded courier is scheduled to arrive in January 2020, the Court will permit the Defendant through and including February 3, 2020, to file a notice with the Court indicating whether or not this mysterious figure has appeared from the shadows and whether the Defendant now has access to the last key slice needed to unlock the encrypted file. In the event this occurs, and further if the Defendant produces his list of Bitcoin Holdings as ordered by the Magistrate Judge, then this Court will not impose any additional sanctions other than the ones discussed above." (p. 22)
  • Four days later Wright's lawyers made the following notice to the court: "Dr. Wright notifies the Court that a third party has provided the necessary information and key slice to unlock the encrypted file, and Dr. Wright has produced a list of his bitcoin holdings, as ordered by the Magistrate Judge, to plaintiffs today."

Independently Verifying the "CSW Filed" List of Addresses

At the very beginning of his analysis, Antonopoulos created a checksum of the "CSW Filed" list. This checksum can be found on p. 21 of his report:

dc29d38e1276c22c4857824e4c5177b0872b32698c3d2f9232d9a1866ec40d91

If you have a csv file with that SHA256 checksum, then you know the content of the CSV is byte-for-byte exactly what Antonopoulos labelled "CSW Filed". Therefore all the addresses in any such csv file are byte-for-byte precisely the list of Bitcoin addresses that Craig Wright submitted to the plaintiffs on 2020-01-14, that he claimed he owned and had been unlocked using the key slice he received from the bonded courier between 2020-01-10 and 2020-01-14. The filename of the document used by Antonopoulous was "DEF_01586024.CSV", but the filename is not significant for creating a checksum.

A .csv file can be created from the 512.7 filing. The Wright-debunker Zectro has reconstructed such a csv file, a copy of which is available here:

The SHA256 checksum for that file is the same as the checksum given by Antonopoulos in his report.

You can calculate the checksum using this online tool (or any other such tool available to you): https://emn178.github.io/online-tools/sha256_checksum.html

If your own checksum from this csv file is identical to the one that Antonopoulos records in his report, then you know for certain that you have precisely the list of addresses that Wright claimed to own, and you have all the links and means above to verify every single step independently of this website.
